## Core components of CharGPT Prompt Part 18 prompts

4.1 Prompt structure and framing (150 words)

- Start with a clear objective: “Generate mood boards for a luxury fragrance brand.”
- Define scope and audience: “Target Instagram followers aged 25–40, urban professionals.”
- Use concrete constraints: “Limit color palettes to 3–4 shades, emphasize teal and emerald.”
- Include expected outputs: mood board sketches, asset inventories, caption ideas.

This structure keeps outputs actionable. When you describe the scene, lighting, and mood exactly, the AI gives you usable prompts to feed into design tools or to brief teammates.

4.2 Role and persona settings (150 words)

- Give the AI a role: “You are a senior design creative director.”
- Specify tone and style: “Professional, concise, with a premium, spa-like vibe.”
- State success criteria: “Outputs should be ready for a design brief, with asset lists and example captions.”
- Layer in constraints: “Avoid jargon, use 6th- to 8th-grade language, and present in bullet-ready form.”

Role-setting helps the AI align with your process, ensuring outputs are drop-in ready for your workflow.

4.3 Constraints and style guidelines (150 words)

- Color and typography: “Provide color palettes with hex codes; suggest two font pairings.”
- Visual rules: “Emphasize high-detail macro shots; keep backgrounds soft and unobtrusive.”
- Copy constraints: “Captions must be short, catchy, and optimized for engagement.”
- Accessibility notes: “Use alt-text-friendly descriptions and legible typography.”

Clear constraints ensure outputs fit your brand and accessibility standards.

## How to craft CharGPT prompts for design ideas

5.1 Brainstorm prompts for mood boards (200 words)

- Prompt idea: “Generate 5 mood-board directions for a luxury fragrance brand with a green, coastal aesthetic. Include 3 color palettes, 4 typography directions, and 2 layout concepts.”
- Add constraints: “Keep the mood board language simple; include one-mentence rationale per direction.”
- Use prompts to trigger visuals: “Describe textures, lighting, and key mood phrases to guide photography briefs.”

5.2 Prompts for concept sketches (200 words)

- Prompt idea: “Provide 3 concept sketch prompts for a perfume bottle redesign. Include silhouette, cap treatment, and label placement.”
- Include references: “Mention ergonomic grip, premium materials, and minimal branding.”
- Output format: “Give a quick sketch layout with notes that can be translated into quick pencil sketches or CAD sketches.”

5.3 Prompts for asset inventories (200 words)

- Prompt idea: “Create an asset inventory for a fragrance launch: logo marks, bottle variants, label styles, packaging mockups, social-ready assets, and UI elements for a landing page.”
- Add outputs: “Provide file types, sizes, and usage notes.”
- Include delivery format: “Provide a checklist that a designer can hand to a production team.”

## Workflow integration and best practices

8.1 Prompt management and organization (170 words)

- Build a prompt library: group prompts by mood boards, assets, captions, and color palettes.
- Version control: track iterations so you can compare outputs and revert to earlier directions.
- Naming conventions: use clear, consistent names like MoodBoard_V1, IconSet_V2.

8.2 Quality checks and revision loops (150 words)

- Always review outputs: check for brand fit, visual coherence, and accessibility.
- Use revision prompts: ask for alternative language, different color emphasis, or varied lighting.
- Schedule feedback rounds with clients: capture their notes, then regenerate with revised prompts.

8.3 Collaboration with clients and stakeholders (180 words)

- Use Part 18 prompts as a collaborative briefing tool: share mood boards and asset inventories to align expectations.
- Create a living prompt brief: update prompts as goals evolve, keeping teams on the same page.
- Document decisions: tie prompts to outcomes so stakeholders can see how prompts influence visuals.

## Advanced tips and pitfalls

9.1 Common pitfalls to avoid (150 words)

- Overfitting prompts to one style: variety is good, but keep core brand cues intact.
- Ignoring accessibility: ensure color contrasts and alt-text are part of prompts.
- Relying on prompts alone: combine with human critique for nuance and originality.

9.2 Tips to scale prompts for design teams (150 words)

- Create a shared prompt library: standardized prompts that new hires can use.
- Use templates for different campaigns: product launches, seasonal campaigns, rebrands.
- Establish a review ritual: weekly prompts review and update based on results.

9.3 Security and copyright considerations (150 words)

- Use prompts that respect client ownership and licensing for assets.
- Avoid reproducing exclusive brand elements without permission.
- Track outputs and edits for auditability in case of disputes.
