## What is Swipe for prompts?

Swipe for prompts is a curated approach to collecting and applying AI prompts across design and marketing tasks. Think of it as a prompt library you can flip through like a deck of cards. Each prompt is crafted to trigger specific ideas, from brand identity to social content. The goal is to turn inspiration into action with minimal friction.

This concept borrows from storytelling prompts, content prompts, and AI prompts used by designers and copywriters. It’s not about replacing human judgment; it’s about giving your brain a fast lane to ideas, iterations, and fast feedback loops. When you have a ready-to-use prompt list, you spend less time wondering what to create and more time shaping great outcomes.

As you build your swipe for prompts library, you’ll notice a pattern: simple inputs yield focused outputs. Clear constraints guide the AI, while room for customization keeps outputs workable for your brand. It’s like having a seasoned creative partner who knows your tone, audience, and goals—without needing a coffee break.

## How to use Swipe for prompts in design workflows

### 4.1 Define goals and scope

Start with a clear objective for the project. Are you refreshing a brand, launching a campaign, or improving a product page? Write down the primary outcomes you want: increased engagement, clearer messaging, faster asset creation, or better accessibility.

Next, define the scope of prompts you’ll use. Create bundles for brand identity, content creation, UX/UI, and research. This helps you stay organized and ensures prompts align with each phase of the project.

### 4.2 Gather and curate prompt sets

Build your prompt library in a simple document or a lightweight tool. Start with a handful of reliable prompts in each category:

- Branding prompts that guide logo concepts, color direction, and typography vibes.
- Content prompts to generate post ideas, captions, and micro-copy.
- UI/UX prompts for layout, components, and accessibility checks.
- Research prompts to summarize competitive landscape and trends.

Curate prompts over time. Keep a log of what worked, what didn’t, and why. This archive becomes your prompt library, a living resource you can refine and reuse.

### 4.3 Apply prompts to assets and briefs

Attach prompts to briefs or assets as you work. For example:

- A brand refresh brief with prompts for logo directions, tagline options, and mood boards.
- A social content sprint with prompts for post ideas, carousels, and captions.
- A product page redesign with prompts for feature storytelling, visuals, and micro-interactions.

Use prompts as starting points, then tailor outputs to your assets. Small adjustments—like tone, audience, or platform—can dramatically improve results.

### 4.4 Iterate, test, and measure results

Treat prompts like experiments. Test variations, compare engagement, and measure speed. Keep track of what prompts yield high-quality outcomes and which need refinement.

A simple testing loop works well:

- Generate outputs with 2–3 prompt variations.
- Compare against a baseline (previous assets, performance metrics).
- Pick the best version, then refine the prompt for even sharper results.

This disciplined approach makes prompt use more effective over time and helps you scale your process across multiple projects.

## Core prompt categories for branding and marketing

### 5.1 Brand identity prompts

- Logo concept prompts that explore mark shapes, wordmark typography, and emblem styles.
- Color direction prompts to generate harmonious palettes aligned with brand personality.
- Typography prompts to balance readability with character and mood.

### 5.2 Content creation prompts

- Storytelling prompts to craft brand stories, customer journeys, and value propositions.
- Social media prompts for evergreen content ideas and timely campaigns.
- Caption prompts to maintain tone and voice across posts.

### 5.3 UX/UI prompts

- Layout prompts to test grid systems and visual hierarchy.
- Component prompts for buttons, cards, and micro-interactions.
- Accessibility prompts to ensure color contrast, keyboard navigation, and readable content.

### 5.4 Research and discovery prompts

- Market scan prompts to summarize competitor positioning.
- Trend prompts to surface emerging design and consumer behavior patterns.

In practice, you’ll rotate through these categories as you move from strategy to execution. The goal is to keep outputs practical and channel-ready. A well-tended prompt library helps teams align faster and push creative boundaries without losing brand integrity.

## Prompt prompts for branding and marketing

### 6.1 Logo and identity prompts

- Generate multiple logo directions with different symbol-and-wordmark combos.
- Propose logo lockups for horizontal and stacked formats.
- Explore brand marks that convey core values in abstract shapes.

### 6.2 Tagline and messaging prompts

- Create 5 tagline options; test for clarity, memorability, and value clarity.
- Draft position statements and one-liners that fit on landing pages.
- Develop brand stories that map to customer pain points and outcomes.

### 6.3 Campaign concept prompts

- Outline 3 campaign concepts with a central hook, visuals, and CTA.
- Propose content cadences for a 2-week launch.
- Suggest media mix and platform-specific twists for Instagram, LinkedIn, and TikTok.

### 6.4 Visual style prompts

- Propose a visual mood (minimal, bold, playful, premium) and translate it into color, typography, and imagery.
- Generate a set of visual prompts for carousel slides and hero banners.
- Create guidance for consistent asset formatting across channels.

## UI/UX prompts for product designers

### 7.1 Layout and grid prompts

- Create wireframes with a 12-column grid and responsive adjustments.
- Produce variations of header, hero, and content blocks to test information density.

### 7.2 Component and micro-interactions prompts

- Design button states, hover effects, and accessible focus indicators.
- Propose micro-interactions that communicate status changes (loading, success, error).

### 7.3 Accessibility and inclusive design prompts

- Check color contrast, text size, and navigability for screen readers.
- Suggest inclusive labeling and alternative text for visuals.

### 7.4 Prototyping and iteration prompts

- Generate rapid prototypes for checking layout feasibility.
- Propose quick usability tests and feedback questions to run with users.

The idea is to keep the prompts practical and repeatable. You should be able to pull a set of prompts, apply them to a design brief, and iterate quickly based on real user feedback.

## Best practices for prompt quality and iteration

### 11.1 Framing prompts effectively

- Be specific about scope, audience, and constraints.
- Include examples to anchor expectations and reduce ambiguity.

### 11.2 Prompt testing and AB testing

- Run controlled tests comparing outputs from two or three prompts.
- Measure outcomes like time saved, engagement change, and perceived quality.

### 11.3 Evaluation metrics

- Use speed (time to first draft), quality (alignment with brief), and reach (engagement, saves, shares) as core metrics.
- Track consistency across assets and channels.

### 11.4 Versioning and maintenance

- Version prompts as you refine them.
- Keep a changelog so teams understand why changes were made.

Prompts improve output when you treat them like code: write, test, refine, and document. The more deliberate you are with framing and metrics, the more value you’ll extract from swipe for prompts.
