## How to design the visuals

A strong chibi capsule design blends character personality, capsule realism, lighting, and slide composition. Start with a clear vision of the character, then layer in glass effects, reflections, and a cohesive color story. The goal is to make the capsule look tangible—like a real toy you might find in a display case—while keeping the chibi cuteness front and center.

In practice, you’ll balance glossy glass with soft shadows, add subtle reflections that suggest a studio setup, and keep the character expressive and approachable. Use a simple background so the capsule stands out, and plan your slide sequence to tell a mini-story across the carousel. The result should feel like a collectible: instantly recognizable, highly shareable, and easy for others to recreate with their own prompts.

### 4.1 Define the character persona

A strong persona guides every design choice. Start by deciding the character’s mood (playful, confident, curious), age impression, and style cues (sporty hoodie, sneakers, tiny gadget). If you want a friendly, approachable vibe, go with big eyes, rounded cheeks, and a slight smile. For a bolder look, give the chibi sharper eyebrows and a dynamic pose. Match the outfit to the brand you want to convey—streetwear vibes work well for social creators who want a modern, relatable feel. This persona becomes the backbone of your prompts, influencing pose, facial expression, and accessories.

### 4.2 Capsule design and glass realism

Glass realism hinges on refraction, subtle highlights, and slight bevels along the capsule edges. Describe a capsule with a gentle curvature, a clear glass finish, and realistic fingerprint-free surfaces. Add tiny reflections of the surrounding environment to imply lighting from a studio or natural daylight. The fingers holding the capsule should show a natural grip, with attention to scale so the chibi interior reads life-size within the glass. If you want a premium look, consider a faint tint to the glass to hint at lighting conditions and help the character pop.

### 4.3 Chibi art prompts

Create prompts that emphasize cute proportions: big head, small body, rounded features. Ask for glossy skin, large expressive eyes, and a tiny headset or gadget to emphasize personality. Include a clear description of wardrobe, color palette, and pose. When drafting prompts, keep sentences short and directive, focusing on the key visual cues you want to appear in every frame. This consistency helps you build a recognizable “chibi capsule” style across multiple slides.

### 4.4 Lighting and glass color

Lighting should be bright but soft to emphasize glossy surfaces and gentle shadows. Use a cool-to-warm balance to keep skin tones natural while letting the red hoodie stand out. Glass color can be kept neutral (clear with slight blue tint) or tinted to harmonize with the character’s outfit. Remember: reflections matter. Small highlights on the capsule surface will sell the 3D feel and help the chibi character look nearly tangible.

### 4.5 Composition for carousel slides

Carousel slides thrive on clear progression. Start with a wide shot of the capsule and fingers, then move to a close-up of the chibi’s face, followed by a detail shot of the tiny hands holding the device, and finish with a reveal or caption slide. Maintain a consistent framing rhythm, so followers anticipate the next slide. Use a simple background and strong contrast between the capsule and backdrop to ensure legibility on small screens.

## How to generate AI art for this concept

Generating AI art for the chibi capsule concept means combining character prompts, capsule prompts, and a cohesive background. Start by outlining the character’s vibe, then the capsule’s properties, and finally the setting. Use a consistent color palette and lighting notes so each slide reads as part of the same scene. You’ll iterate with variations to find the exact mix that gets the most engagement on social media.

### 5.1 Prompt framework for the character

Character prompts should include: style (chibi, 3D chibi model), mood, clothing, and props. Example elements: big eyes, rosy cheeks, red Nike hoodie, blue Levi jeans, Nike sneakers, and a handheld tablet or iPad. Include a clear action or pose (standing with a device, gentle smile) and a short background descriptor to keep the focus on the face and outfit.

### 5.2 Prompt framework for the capsule

Capsule prompts must specify the all-glass gashapon design, size relative to fingers, lighting, and reflections. Mention glass thickness, edge bevels, and subtle tint. Indicate “held between two fingers” and ensure the capsule reads as premium, with clean, smooth curvature and realistic transparency.

### 5.3 Prompt framework for the background

Background prompts can be minimal to avoid competing with the capsule. Use a soft studio backdrop or a simple gradient that complements the color palette. Add a light vignette to focus attention on the capsule. If you want context, suggest a hint of a desk or shelf without clutter.

### 5.4 Style and mood variations

Experiment with multiple moods: cute and glossy, soft pastel, high-contrast vector, or watercolor-like. Try different palettes: bold primary colors, candy pastels, or a chic monochrome with a pop of red from the hoodie. Each variation helps you test what resonates with your audience.

### 5.5 Iteration tips and prompts

Start with a base prompt, then refine: adjust lighting, tweak glass tint, alter pose, and experiment with background brightness. Use negative prompts to avoid unwanted elements (e.g., no text on the capsule, no heavy shadows). Save prompts that perform best and remix them with new outfits or accessories to keep content fresh.

### 10.1 Accessibility and alt text

### 10.2 Copyright and licensing for AI art

Be clear about usage rights, especially for AI-generated imagery. Respect platform policies and any external assets used in prompts.

### 10.3 Avoiding content fatigue
