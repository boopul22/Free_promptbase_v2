## 4. Key prompt categories for wedding pros

### 4.1 Visual prompts for ads and mood boards (120 words)

Visual prompts are the backbone. They describe scenes, lighting, composition, and mood so designers can reproduce consistent looks across campaigns. For wedding ads, you might specify: “soft dawn lighting, pastel florals, elegant serif typography, warm skin tones, and a shallow depth of field.” Use these prompts to create mood boards for venues, decor styles, and invitations. Visual prompts also help when you want to generate alternate versions to test in social feeds.

### 4.2 Copy prompts for captions and taglines (100 words)

Copy prompts guide your voice. Create variations for captions like “Your day, your vows, your story,” or “Where love and laughter meet forever.” Include prompts for taglines, wedding hashtags, and micro-copy for ads. You can tailor copy to platforms (Instagram, Facebook, Pinterest) and audiences (couples, families, planners). For example, a prompt could request a warm, celebratory tone for wedding reception promos or a refined, timeless voice for luxury venues. Pair copy prompts with visuals to maintain a cohesive campaign.

### 4.3 Brand and style prompts (90 words)

Brand prompts lock in style: typography, color ramps, logos, and patterns. A single style prompt can prescribe a decorative motif (elephant motifs in borders, gold filigree), a color system (rose gold, blush, ivory), and a typography vibe (serif with a modern sans). When used consistently, these prompts ensure your wedding content feels like “one brand” across invitations, social ads, and venue signage.

### 4.4 Mood and color palette prompts (90 words)

Mood prompts specify emotions (romantic, grand, intimate) and atmospheres (sunset glow, candlelit, airy garden). Color palette prompts guide combinations like champagne golds with dusty pinks or deep maroons with brass accents. Together, mood and color prompts help you generate visuals that resonate with couples while staying on-brand.

## 5. How to implement CharGPT prompts in workflows

### 5.1 Prompt templates for designers (120 words)

Create reusable templates for visual concepts, caption variations, and color palettes. For example:
- Visual concept template: scene, lighting, subject, background, and mood.
- Caption template: tone, length, call-to-action, and platform.
- Color palette template: primary, secondary, and accent swatches with usage notes.

Store templates in a shared doc or design system so team members can copy, customize, and publish quickly. Train junior designers with a quick walk-through of how prompts map to assets.

### 5.2 Collaboration between planners and designers (100 words)

Prompts become collaboration briefs. Planners can describe the event vibe, venue, and audience, then designers translate that into AI prompts. Quick review loops with a shared feedback checklist keep outputs aligned with client goals. Encourage teams to annotate prompts with brand notes and usage rights to avoid misalignment later.

### 5.3 QA and prompts testing (100 words)

Test prompts on a small batch before large campaigns. Check for brand consistency, visual coherence, and factual accuracy in text (names, venues, dates). Create a checklist: color accuracy, typography, image realism, and accessibility (alt text, contrast). A/B test variations to learn what resonates.

### 5.4 Automation and asset generation pipelines (180 words)

Automate repetitive parts of the flow. Use tools like Zapier or Make to trigger AI prompts from a form (client brief), push outputs to a shared folder, and sync captions to social media calendars. Build pipelines for: mood boards → visuals → copy → ad variants → social posts. Use versioning so you can roll back if a prompt generates an off-brand result. This keeps your process scalable and repeatable.

## 7. Best practices and ethics

- Respect client rights: ensure you own or have rights to the prompts and assets generated. Use license-compliant outputs for client campaigns.
- Maintain authenticity: AI should support, not replace, human storytelling. Add personal storytelling from vows or real moments to keep content genuine.
- Avoid stereotypes: tailor prompts to reflect diverse cultures, languages, and traditions with sensitivity.
- Ensure accessibility: add alt text to images, choose readable fonts, and maintain contrast for readability.
- Validate outputs: verify dates, venues, and facts before publishing. Prompt QA is essential to avoid errors.
